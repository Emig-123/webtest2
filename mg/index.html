Read the full background

Metallgesellschaft's US subsidiary, MG Refining & Marketing (MGRM), sold long-dated fixed-price fuel supply contracts to customers — some running 5 to 10 years — and hedged that exposure with a "stack-and-roll" program: buying large stacks of short-dated NYMEX futures and rolling them forward every month, rather than using illiquid long-dated contracts. At its peak the position was reportedly large enough to represent a meaningful share of total open interest in crude and heating oil futures, which made it hard to unwind quickly once trouble started. The strategy depended on the futures curve staying in backwardation (near-term prices above deferred prices), so each monthly roll would produce a small profit.

When oil prices fell sharply in late 1993 and the curve flipped into contango, the stack-and-roll program lost money on every roll — and because futures are marked to market daily, MGRM faced enormous margin calls in cash immediately, while the offsetting gains on its fixed-price delivery contracts wouldn't be realized for years. In other words, the hedge was sized and timed for the wrong horizon: a short-dated instrument standing in for a long-dated exposure. That mismatch turned a paper hedge into a real cash crisis, and Metallgesellschaft's supervisory board unwound the position in December 1993 at a widely reported loss of roughly $1.3 billion (other estimates run higher once related contract cancellations are included).

Economists still disagree on how to characterize it. Mello & Parsons (1995) argue the maturity mismatch between the short-dated hedge and the long-dated exposure was itself the core design flaw — in effect, a large directional bet on the shape of the futures curve. Culp & Miller (1995) counter that the hedge ratio was reasonable and the program was fundamentally sound; the real mistake was the supervisory board panicking and liquidating the position at the worst possible time, converting a temporary cash-flow squeeze into a permanent loss. Either way, the case is a standard illustration of how a hedge that looks right in aggregate size but wrong in timing can become the source of the risk it was meant to remove — the heatmap below shows the same tension: outcomes are sensitive to both the assumed futures price and the hedge ratio chosen, and a larger hedge does not uniformly reduce exposure, it changes what you're exposed to.

Further reading:
  • Mello, A. & Parsons, J. (1995). "Maturity Structure of a Hedge Matters: Lessons from the Metallgesellschaft Debacle," Journal of Applied Corporate Finance, 8(1).
  • Culp, C. & Miller, M. (1995). "Metallgesellschaft and the Economics of Synthetic Storage," Journal of Applied Corporate Finance, 7(4).

About this simulation

Front Price for each of the 12 months was drawn once in the source workbook via NORM.INV(RAND(), Fixed Price, Volatility) and frozen as this page's baseline scenario — reloading the page does not redraw the random path. Roll P&L, Total Roll P&L, Delivery P&L, and Total Delivery P&L replicate the StacknRoll Strategy Analysis sheet's formulas exactly. The Hedge Ratio Analysis grid (RollProfit, LaR_h, ShortFall, FundingCost, NetProfit) and the Sensitivity Table's per-cell formula are reproduced the same way, including the workbook's blank Delivery reference cell (which evaluates to $0 in the source file and is kept that way here rather than "fixed").

  • Price Risk, Basis Risk, Liquidity Risk, and the Liquidity Squeeze Test sheets are separate probability-distribution exercises and are out of scope for this page; the two values they feed into this chain (Volatility, LAR) are frozen constants extracted from the source workbook.
  • All 420 cells are computed client-side from the same frozen inputs — nothing here is randomized on load or on interaction.
  • The scenario badge above exists so any future "generate a new path" control can't be mistaken for live or historical market data — this is one simulated draw, not a forecast.
